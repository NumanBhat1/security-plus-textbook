## 4. Concept Overview

Incident response (IR) is the structured way to handle a security incident — the SOC's core mission. The exam tests the **7-step lifecycle in order** (lots of "what's the FIRST/NEXT step?" questions), the **tabletop vs simulation** distinction, and **digital forensics** (especially **chain of custody**).

```
   Preparation → Detection → Analysis → Containment → Eradication → Recovery → Lessons Learned
```

---

## 5. Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Preparation** | Building the IR capability before incidents (plans, tools, training). |
| **Detection** | Identifying that an incident is occurring. |
| **Analysis** | Investigating scope/impact/nature. |
| **Containment** | Limiting the incident's spread. |
| **Eradication** | Removing the threat (malware, accounts, persistence). |
| **Recovery** | Restoring systems to normal operation. |
| **Lessons learned** | Post-incident review to improve. |
| **Tabletop exercise** | Discussion-based IR walkthrough. |

| **Root cause analysis (RCA)** | Finding the underlying cause. |
| **Threat hunting** | Proactively searching for hidden threats. |
| **Legal hold** | Preserving data for litigation. |

| **Preservation** | Keeping evidence unaltered. |
| **E-discovery** | Identifying/collecting electronic data for legal use. |

## 6. Deep Technical Explanation

### The 7-step IR process (memorize the order)

| # | Step | What happens |
|---|------|--------------|
| 1 | **Preparation** | Plans, tools, training **before** anything happens |
| 2 | **Detection** | Identify a potential incident (alerts, reports) |
| 3 | **Analysis** | Confirm + assess scope/impact |
| 4 | **Containment** | Stop the spread (isolate hosts, block) |
| 5 | **Eradication** | Remove the threat (malware, backdoors, bad accounts) |
| 6 | **Recovery** | Restore systems to normal (and monitor) |
| 7 | **Lessons learned** | Review; improve for next time |

> **Order traps (very common):**
> - **First step overall = Preparation** (before incidents).
> - **Containment comes BEFORE eradication** (stop the bleeding before cleaning up).
> - **Eradication before recovery** (remove the threat before restoring).
> - **Lessons learned is LAST.**
> "What's the FIRST thing to do when an incident is detected?" — after detection, you **analyze**, then **contain**. (Preparation is the first *lifecycle* phase but happens *before* the incident.)

### Training & testing

- **Training** — prepare the team (roles, playbooks).
- **Testing:**
  - **Tabletop exercise** — **discussion-based** walkthrough (low cost, no system impact).
  - **Simulation** — a **realistic drill** (more involved/realistic).

> **Tabletop = talk through it; simulation = act it out (realistic).** (Same distinction as 3.4/5.5.)

### Root cause analysis (RCA)

After containment/eradication, find the **underlying cause** (not just symptoms) so it doesn't recur — feeds lessons learned.

### Threat hunting

**Proactively** searching for threats that evaded detection (hypothesis-driven, using IOCs/TTPs) — *before* an alert fires. Contrast with reactive IR (which starts after detection).

> **Threat hunting = proactive search** (assume compromise, go look). IR = reactive (responds to detection).

### Digital forensics

Collecting and handling **evidence** properly so it's usable (legally and technically):

| Term | What it is |
|------|-----------|
| **Legal hold** | Formal duty to **preserve** data for litigation |
| **Chain of custody** | Documented record of **who handled evidence, when, and how** |
| **Acquisition** | **Collecting** evidence (forensic disk/memory imaging) |
| **Preservation** | Keeping evidence **unaltered** (hashes, write-blockers) |
| **Reporting** | Documenting findings |
| **E-discovery** | Identifying/collecting **electronic data** for legal proceedings |

> **Chain of custody is the most-tested forensics term:** it documents every person who handled evidence and every transfer — broken chain = evidence inadmissible. **Order of volatility** (collect most volatile first — RAM before disk) is a related forensics concept.

---

## 7. Why It Exists

Incidents are inevitable; a structured response **limits damage, speeds recovery, preserves evidence, and drives improvement**. Without a plan, response is chaotic, evidence is destroyed, and the same incident recurs. Forensics ensures evidence holds up legally; lessons learned closes the loop.

---

## 8. How It Works (enterprise)

A CSIRT follows the **IR plan**: detect (SIEM/EDR), analyze, **contain** (isolate), **eradicate** (remove threat), **recover** (restore + monitor), and run **lessons learned + RCA**. Evidence is handled with **chain of custody**, **legal hold**, and proper **acquisition/preservation**. Teams **train** and run **tabletop/simulation** tests. **Threat hunting** proactively looks for what detection missed.

---

## 9. Advantages

- **Limited damage + faster recovery** (structured steps).
- **Preserved, admissible evidence** (forensics).
- **Continuous improvement** (lessons learned + RCA).
- **Proactive detection** (threat hunting).
- **Readiness** (training + testing).

---

## 10. Disadvantages / Challenges

- **Requires preparation/resources** (plans, tools, skilled people).
- **Evidence handling is strict** (broken chain of custody = unusable).
- **Pressure/time** during live incidents.
- **Testing takes effort** (but untested plans fail).

---

## 11. Real-World Examples

- **Containment before eradication:** isolate infected hosts (contain), then remove the malware (eradicate).
- **Chain of custody:** every transfer of a seized laptop is logged and signed.
- **Tabletop:** the team walks through a ransomware scenario over a meeting.
- **Threat hunting:** an analyst hunts for a known APT's TTPs and finds dormant persistence.
- **RCA:** the breach traced to an unpatched VPN (root cause), not just the malware (symptom).

---

## 12. Enterprise Usage (SOC relevance)

This **is** the SOC mission:
- You **detect → analyze → contain** daily.
- You preserve evidence with **chain of custody**.
- You participate in **tabletops/simulations** and **threat hunts**.
- You contribute to **lessons learned / RCA**.
- Recovery depends on tested **backups** (3.4).

---

## 13. Security Implications

- **No containment-first** = the incident spreads.
- **Skipping eradication** = the threat persists (reinfection).
- **Broken chain of custody** = evidence thrown out.
- **No lessons learned/RCA** = repeat incidents.
- **No threat hunting** = dwell time stays long.

---

## 14. Attacker / Adversary Perspective

- Attackers **destroy logs/evidence** to break investigations (anti-forensics).
- They establish **persistence** (eradication must remove it, not just the malware).
- They rely on **long dwell time** — threat hunting counters this.
- They move fast — **containment speed** matters.

---

## 15. Defender Perspective

- **Prepare** (plan, tools, training) before incidents.
- **Contain before eradicate; eradicate before recover.**
- **Preserve evidence** (chain of custody, legal hold, proper acquisition).
- **Run RCA + lessons learned;** fix root causes.
- **Threat hunt** proactively; **test** the plan (tabletop/simulation).

---

## 16. Detection / Tooling

- **SIEM/EDR/XDR** for detection.
- **Forensic tools** (imaging, write-blockers, hashing).
- **Threat-hunting platforms** (IOC/TTP search).
- **Playbooks/SOAR** to drive consistent response.

---

## 17. Best Practices

1. **Maintain and test an IR plan** (tabletop + simulation).
2. **Follow the order:** contain → eradicate → recover.
3. **Preserve evidence** with chain of custody + legal hold.
4. **Do RCA + lessons learned** after every incident.
5. **Threat hunt** proactively.
6. **Train the team** regularly.

## 19. Diagrams

**IR lifecycle:**

```
   PREPARATION → DETECTION → ANALYSIS → CONTAINMENT → ERADICATION → RECOVERY → LESSONS LEARNED
   (before)                              (stop spread)  (remove)     (restore)   (improve)
```

**Containment-first rule:**

```
   Detect → Analyze → CONTAIN (stop the bleeding) → ERADICATE (clean up) → RECOVER (restore)
   ✗ never eradicate/recover before containing
```

**Chain of custody:**

```
   Evidence collected → logged (who/what/when/where) → each transfer signed → preserved (hash)
   broken chain = evidence inadmissible
```
