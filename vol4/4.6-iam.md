## 4. Concept Overview

**IAM** answers two questions: *who are you?* (authentication/identity) and *what can you do?* (authorization/access). It's a huge SOC/security area. The exam-heavy parts: the **access control models** (MAC vs DAC vs RBAC vs ABAC — constantly confused), the **MFA factors**, the **SSO protocols (SAML/OAuth/LDAP)**, and **PAM** concepts.

```
   Identity proofing → Provision account → Authenticate (MFA) → Authorize (access model)
   → Maintain (attestation, least privilege) → De-provision (offboarding)
```

## 6. Deep Technical Explanation

### Account lifecycle

- **Provisioning** — create the account with correct access (ideally automated, least privilege).
- **De-provisioning** — **remove access promptly at offboarding** (orphaned accounts are a major risk).
- **Permission assignments** — grant rights; over-permissioning → bigger blast radius.
- **Identity proofing** — verify identity **before** issuing credentials (e.g., document checks).
- **Attestation** — periodic **access reviews** confirming users still need their access (recertification).

### Federation & SSO

- **Federation** — trust identities **across organizations** (your identity at company A logs you into partner B).
- **SSO** — one authentication grants access to **many** systems.
- **The protocols:**

| Protocol | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| **SAML** | **Authentication/SSO** (XML) between Identity Provider (IdP) and Service Provider — common for enterprise web SSO |
| **OAuth** | **Authorization** (delegated access) — "allow this app to access my data" (often with OIDC for auth) |
| **LDAP** | **Directory** protocol — query/authenticate against a directory (e.g., Active Directory) |

> **SAML vs OAuth trap:** **SAML = authentication/SSO (who you are); OAuth = authorization (delegated access to resources).** "Enterprise web SSO with XML assertions" = SAML; "let an app access my account on my behalf" = OAuth. **LDAP = the directory** you authenticate against.

### Access control models (the most-confused set)

| Model | Who decides access | Tell |
|-------|--------------------|------|
| **MAC** (Mandatory) | **The system**, by **labels/clearances** (rigid) | "labels, classification, government/military" |
| **DAC** (Discretionary) | **The data owner** decides (flexible) | "owner grants access at their discretion" |
| **RBAC** (Role-Based) | By **job role** | "access based on role/job function" |
| **Rule-Based** | By **rules/conditions** (e.g., firewall ACLs, time rules) | "if-then rules apply to everyone" |
| **ABAC** (Attribute-Based) | By **attributes** (user/resource/environment) | "based on attributes — dept, location, time" |

> **MAC vs DAC:** MAC = **system enforces labels** (you can't change it); DAC = **owner decides** (discretion). **RBAC vs ABAC:** RBAC = by **role**; ABAC = by **attributes** (finer-grained: department + location + time).
> **Time-of-day restrictions** and **least privilege** are additional controls layered on top.

### Multifactor authentication (MFA)

**Factors (must be different TYPES):**

| Factor | Examples |
|--------|----------|
| **Something you know** | Password, PIN |
| **Something you have** | Token, security key, phone |
| **Something you are** | Biometric (fingerprint, face) |
| **Somewhere you are** | Location/geolocation |

**Implementations:** biometrics, **hard tokens** (physical), **soft tokens** (app-generated codes), **security keys** (FIDO2).

> **MFA = 2+ DIFFERENT factor types.** Two passwords ≠ MFA (same type). Password + fingerprint = MFA (know + are).

### Password best practices

| Concept | Guidance |
|---------|----------|
| **Length** | Longer is stronger (most important factor) |
| **Complexity** | Mix of character types |
| **Reuse** | Don't reuse passwords |
| **Expiration / Age** | Periodic change (modern guidance favors length + no forced rotation unless compromised) |
| **Password managers** | Generate/store unique strong passwords |
| **Passwordless** | Authenticate without a password (biometrics, security keys, passkeys) |

> Modern guidance (NIST): **length > complexity**; avoid forced frequent rotation; use **password managers** and move toward **passwordless/MFA**.

### Privileged Access Management (PAM)

Controls **privileged/admin accounts** (the highest-risk):

| Concept | What it is |
|---------|-----------|
| **Just-in-time (JIT)** | Grant elevated access **only when needed**, temporarily |
| **Password vaulting** | Store privileged credentials in a secure vault (check-out/check-in) |
| **Ephemeral credentials** | Short-lived credentials that expire quickly |

> PAM minimizes standing privilege — admins get access **just-in-time**, from a **vault**, with **ephemeral** (short-lived) credentials, reducing the window of abuse.

---

## 7. Why It Exists

Identity is the new perimeter — most breaches involve stolen/abused credentials. IAM exists to ensure **only the right people get the right access, for only as long as needed**, and that it's verified (proofing), reviewed (attestation), and removed promptly (de-provisioning). MFA and PAM exist because passwords alone and standing admin rights are the biggest credential risks.

---

## 8. How It Works (enterprise)

Identities are **proofed**, **provisioned** (least privilege), and authenticated via **SSO/federation (SAML/OAuth/LDAP)** with **MFA**. Authorization uses an **access model** (RBAC/ABAC commonly). **Attestation** reviews keep access current; **de-provisioning** removes it at offboarding. **PAM** governs admin access (JIT, vaulting, ephemeral). Everything is logged to the SIEM (account anomalies → 4.4/UEBA).

---

## 9. Advantages

- **Right access, right people** (least privilege + models).
- **Stronger auth** (MFA defeats most credential attacks).
- **Convenience + control** (SSO/federation).
- **Reduced privileged risk** (PAM/JIT).
- **Ongoing correctness** (attestation, de-provisioning).

---

## 10. Disadvantages / Challenges

- **SSO = single point** (one compromise → many systems → mitigate with MFA).
- **Complexity** (federation, ABAC policies).
- **Orphaned accounts** if de-provisioning lags.
- **MFA friction/fatigue** (push-bombing attacks).
- **Over-permissioning drift** without attestation.

---

## 11. Real-World Examples

- **SAML SSO:** log into Salesforce via the company IdP.
- **OAuth:** "Allow this app to access your Google contacts."
- **RBAC:** all "HR" role members get HR-system access.
- **ABAC:** access only if dept=Finance AND location=HQ AND time=business hours.
- **MFA:** password + a security key (know + have).
- **PAM/JIT:** an admin requests elevated access for 1 hour, auto-revoked after.
- **Orphaned account:** a departed employee's account still active → de-provisioning failure.

---

## 12. Enterprise Usage (SOC relevance)

- **Account anomalies** (impossible travel, concurrent sessions, MFA fatigue) are core SOC alerts (UEBA — 4.5).
- **Orphaned/over-privileged accounts** are findings you report.
- **PAM logs** track privileged use (high-value monitoring).
- **Failed-auth / lockout** patterns = brute force/spraying (2.4).
- You verify **least privilege** and **de-provisioning** during investigations.

---

## 13. Security Implications

- **Stolen credentials** are the top breach vector — **MFA** is the key defense.
- **Orphaned accounts** = backdoors.
- **Over-privilege** = bigger blast radius.
- **SSO without MFA** = one password compromises everything.
- **Standing admin rights** = prime target (→ PAM/JIT).

---

## 14. Attacker / Adversary Perspective

- Attackers **steal/guess credentials** (phishing, spraying, stuffing).
- They target **accounts without MFA** and **orphaned accounts**.
- They abuse **over-privileged** and **standing admin** access.
- They use **MFA fatigue/push-bombing** to bypass weak MFA.
- They pivot through **SSO** once they have one identity.

---

## 15. Defender Perspective

- **Enforce MFA everywhere** (phishing-resistant: security keys/FIDO2).
- **Least privilege + RBAC/ABAC;** review via **attestation**.
- **De-provision promptly** at offboarding (no orphans).
- **Use PAM** (JIT, vaulting, ephemeral) for admins.
- **Identity-proof** before issuing credentials.
- **Monitor account behavior** (UEBA).

---

## 16. Detection / Tooling

- **UEBA** for account anomalies (impossible travel, concurrent sessions).
- **PAM tools** logging privileged use.
- **SIEM** for auth failures/lockouts.
- **Access reviews/attestation** reports for drift.
- **Identity providers** (SSO logs).

---

## 17. Mitigation Techniques

- MFA (phishing-resistant); least privilege; RBAC/ABAC.
- PAM (JIT, vaulting, ephemeral credentials).
- Prompt de-provisioning; periodic attestation.
- Password managers / passwordless; identity proofing.

---

## 18. Best Practices

1. **Enforce MFA** (prefer security keys/passwordless).
2. **Least privilege;** use **RBAC/ABAC**; **attest** access periodically.
3. **De-provision immediately** at offboarding.
4. **Use PAM** (JIT, vaulting, ephemeral) for privileged accounts.
5. **Identity-proof** before issuing credentials.
6. **Password managers** + length-first policies; move toward **passwordless**.

## 20. Diagrams

**IAM lifecycle:**

```
   identity proofing → provision (least privilege) → authenticate (MFA via SSO)
   → authorize (MAC/DAC/RBAC/ABAC) → attest (review) → de-provision (offboarding)
```

**MFA = different types:**

```
   know (password) + have (security key) = MFA ✓
   password + PIN = NOT MFA (both "know") ✗
```

**MAC vs DAC vs RBAC vs ABAC:**

```
   MAC  = system labels decide (rigid)
   DAC  = owner decides (discretion)
   RBAC = your ROLE decides
   ABAC = your ATTRIBUTES decide (dept+location+time)
```

- **Attestation = periodic access review/recertification.**

- **Least privilege = minimum access.**
- Modern passwords: **length > complexity**; password managers; passwordless.

## 22. Common CompTIA Question Styles

1. *"Access is enforced by the system using security labels and clearances."* → MAC.
2. *"The data owner decides who can access their files."* → DAC.
3. *"Access is granted based on job role."* → RBAC.
4. *"Access depends on attributes like department, location, and time."* → ABAC.
5. *"Which provides SSO using XML assertions between an IdP and service provider?"* → SAML.
6. *"Which delegates authorization, letting an app access resources on a user's behalf?"* → OAuth.
7. *"Password + fingerprint is an example of…"* → MFA (something you know + something you are).
8. *"Granting admin rights only temporarily when needed is…"* → Just-in-time (PAM).

**Why distractors fail:** they swap MAC/DAC/RBAC/ABAC or SAML/OAuth. Anchor on *who decides* (system/owner/role/attributes) and *auth vs authz*.

## 23. Interview Notes

- *"MAC vs DAC?"* — MAC: the system enforces access via labels (rigid); DAC: the owner decides (discretionary).
- *"RBAC vs ABAC?"* — RBAC by role; ABAC by attributes (finer-grained: dept/location/time).
- *"SAML vs OAuth?"* — SAML = authentication/SSO; OAuth = authorization (delegated access).
- *"What counts as MFA?"* — Two or more different factor types (know/have/are/somewhere).
- *"What is PAM/JIT?"* — Managing privileged access; JIT grants elevation only temporarily when needed.

## 24. Common Beginner Mistakes

1. **Confusing MAC/DAC/RBAC/ABAC.**
2. **Swapping SAML (authn) and OAuth (authz).**
3. **Thinking two same-type factors = MFA.**
4. **Forgetting de-provisioning** (orphaned accounts).
5. **Confusing attestation (review) with authentication.**
6. **Confusing identity proofing with provisioning.**
7. **Over-valuing complexity over length** in passwords.

## 29. Practice Questions

**Q1.** Access decisions are enforced by the operating system using security labels and clearances that users cannot change. This is:
A. DAC  B. MAC  C. RBAC  D. ABAC

**Q2.** A file's owner personally decides who may access it. This is:
A. MAC  B. DAC  C. RBAC  D. Rule-based

**Q3.** Users receive access based on their job function (e.g., "Accountant"). This is:
A. ABAC  B. MAC  C. RBAC  D. DAC

**Q4.** Access is granted only if the user's department is "Finance," location is "HQ," and the time is business hours. This is:
A. RBAC  B. MAC  C. ABAC  D. DAC

**Q5.** Which protocol provides web SSO using XML assertions between an identity provider and a service provider?
A. OAuth  B. LDAP  C. SAML  D. RADIUS

**Q6.** Which framework allows an application to access a user's resources on their behalf (delegated authorization)?
A. SAML  B. OAuth  C. Kerberos  D. SPF

**Q7.** A login requires a password and a fingerprint. This is an example of:
A. Two-step same-factor  B. Multifactor authentication (know + are)  C. SSO  D. Federation

**Q8.** Granting an administrator elevated rights only temporarily, exactly when needed, is:
A. Standing privilege  B. Just-in-time access  C. DAC  D. Federation

**Q9.** Periodically reviewing and recertifying that users still need their access is called:
A. Provisioning  B. Attestation  C. Identity proofing  D. Federation

**Q10.** Which is the BIGGEST risk of failing to de-provision accounts at offboarding?
A. Slower logins  B. Orphaned accounts that remain active as backdoors  C. Higher licensing cost only  D. Nothing

### Answers
- **Q1 — B** (MAC). **Q2 — B** (DAC). **Q3 — C** (RBAC). **Q4 — C** (ABAC). **Q5 — C** (SAML). **Q6 — B** (OAuth). **Q7 — B** (MFA). **Q8 — B** (JIT). **Q9 — B** (Attestation). **Q10 — B** (Orphaned accounts).
