# Volume 2 — Domain 2.0: Threats, Vulnerabilities, and Mitigations

# Objective 2.1 — Compare and contrast common threat actors and motivations

> **Official CompTIA Objective Statement (SY0-701, v5.0):**
> *Compare and contrast common threat actors and motivations.*
>
> Official sub-bullets:
> - **Threat actors** — Nation-state; Unskilled attacker; Hacktivist; Insider threat; Organized crime; Shadow IT
> - **Attributes of actors** — Internal/external; Resources/funding; Level of sophistication/capability
> - **Motivations** — Data exfiltration; Espionage; Service disruption; Blackmail; Financial gain; Philosophical/political beliefs; Ethical; Revenge; Disruption/chaos; War

---

## 3. Learning Outcomes

After this objective you will be able to:

1. Identify and contrast the six **threat actor types** (nation-state, unskilled attacker, hacktivist, insider threat, organized crime, shadow IT).
2. Describe the **attributes** that differentiate actors (internal/external, resources/funding, sophistication).
3. Match **motivations** to the actors most associated with them.
4. Recognize the actor/motivation in a scenario question (CompTIA's favorite format here).

---

## 4. Concept Overview

A **threat actor** (or *malicious actor*) is any entity — person, group, or organization — that is responsible for an event or incident that impacts the security of another entity. Understanding *who* attacks and *why* is foundational: it shapes the threats you'll face, the controls you prioritize, and how you interpret indicators during an investigation.

This objective is almost entirely about **comparison** — CompTIA wants you to read a short scenario ("a well-funded group backed by a government steals defense research") and instantly name the actor (nation-state) and motivation (espionage). So the highest-value skill here is associating each actor with its typical **attributes** (resources, sophistication, internal/external) and **motivations**.

Three clusters:

1. **Threat actors** — the six types and what defines each.
2. **Attributes** — the dimensions used to compare them (internal/external, resources/funding, sophistication).
3. **Motivations** — the "why," and which actors pair with which motives.

## 6. Deep Technical Explanation

### The six threat actors

| Actor | Internal/External | Resources | Sophistication | Typical motivation |
|-------|-------------------|-----------|----------------|--------------------|
| **Nation-state** | External | Very high (gov-funded) | Very high (APT) | Espionage, war, service disruption |
| **Organized crime** | External | High | High | Financial gain |
| **Hacktivist** | External | Low–moderate | Moderate | Philosophical/political beliefs |
| **Insider threat** | **Internal** | Varies | Varies | Revenge, financial gain, espionage |
| **Unskilled attacker** | External | Low | **Low** | Disruption/chaos, "for fun" |
| **Shadow IT** | **Internal** | Varies | Varies | Convenience (not malicious — but risky) |

**Nation-state** — Government-sponsored, the most dangerous: effectively unlimited resources, top sophistication, and patience. Usually **APTs** that stay hidden for months/years. Motivated by **espionage**, **war**, and **service disruption** (attacking critical infrastructure). The phrase "highly sophisticated and well-funded" almost always points here.

**Organized crime** — Professional criminal enterprises run like businesses. High resources and skill, but the goal is **money** (ransomware, banking fraud, stolen data sold on the dark web). "Financially motivated, professional, well-organized" = organized crime.

**Hacktivist** — Driven by a **cause** (political, social, ideological), not money. Typical actions: defacing websites, DDoS, leaking documents to embarrass a target. Resources/skill are usually lower than nation-states or organized crime. "Promoting a political/social agenda" = hacktivist.

**Insider threat** — The actor is **inside** the org (employee, contractor, partner) with legitimate access. Can be **malicious** (revenge, financial gain, selling secrets) or **unintentional** (negligent — though SY0-701 lists insider under threat actors with malicious framing). Dangerous because they bypass the perimeter and know where the valuables are. "A disgruntled employee" = insider threat.

**Unskilled attacker** — Low skill, uses **pre-made tools/scripts** written by others (the term replaces "script kiddie"). Motivated by curiosity, bragging rights, or chaos. Low resources and sophistication. "Uses downloaded tools without understanding them" = unskilled attacker.

**Shadow IT** — Not an outside hacker at all: it's employees using **unauthorized** hardware/software/cloud services (e.g., a team using a personal Dropbox or an unsanctioned SaaS app). The "threat" is the *uncontrolled, unmonitored, unpatched* attack surface it creates — bypassing IT's security. "Employees deploying unsanctioned cloud apps" = shadow IT.

### Attributes of actors (the comparison dimensions)

CompTIA compares actors along three axes:

- **Internal vs. external** — Insiders and shadow IT are **internal**; the rest are typically **external**.
- **Resources/funding** — Nation-states (highest) and organized crime (high) vs. hacktivists and unskilled attackers (low).
- **Level of sophistication/capability** — Nation-states/APTs (highest) vs. unskilled attackers (lowest).

> These three attributes are exactly how a scenario question lets you identify the actor: "well-funded + highly sophisticated + external" → nation-state; "low-skill + uses others' tools" → unskilled attacker.

### Motivations (the "why")

| Motivation | Most associated actor(s) |
|------------|--------------------------|
| **Financial gain** | Organized crime; some insiders |
| **Espionage** | Nation-state; competitors; insiders |
| **Data exfiltration** | Nation-state, organized crime, insiders |
| **Service disruption** | Nation-state, hacktivists |
| **Philosophical/political beliefs** | Hacktivists |
| **Revenge** | Insider threats |
| **Blackmail** | Organized crime (e.g., ransomware + data leak threats) |
| **Disruption/chaos** | Unskilled attackers, hacktivists |
| **War** | Nation-state |
| **Ethical** | Authorized/white-hat researchers (not malicious) |

> **Ethical** stands out — it's the motivation of authorized security researchers/pen testers improving defenses, not an attacker harming you. CompTIA includes it to contrast with malicious motives.

## 7. Why It Exists

This objective exists because **defense starts with knowing your adversary**. You allocate controls differently depending on who realistically targets you: a small business worries about organized crime (ransomware) and unskilled attackers; a defense contractor worries about nation-state espionage. Threat modeling, risk assessment (5.2), and threat intelligence all depend on accurately characterizing actors and motivations.

---

## 8. How It Works (enterprise)

Security teams use **threat intelligence** to profile which actors target their industry, then map those actors' typical **TTPs** (tactics, techniques, procedures) to their defenses. A bank models organized-crime fraud; a hospital models ransomware crews; a government agency models nation-state APTs. Insider-threat programs (UEBA, least privilege, monitoring) address the internal actors; shadow-IT discovery tools (CASB, network scanning) find unsanctioned systems. Motivation informs likely targets — espionage actors go for IP, financial actors go for payment data.

---

## 9. Advantages (of understanding actors/motivations)

- **Prioritized defense** — focus controls on the actors who realistically target you.
- **Better detection** — knowing an actor's TTPs sharpens your alerts.
- **Informed risk assessment** — actor capability feeds likelihood (5.2).
- **Faster IR** — recognizing the likely actor speeds attribution and response.

---

## 10. Disadvantages / Limitations

- **Attribution is hard** — actors hide and use false flags; guessing wrong misdirects response.
- **Overlap** — the same motivation (e.g., data exfiltration) fits multiple actors.
- **Evolving actors** — lines blur (nation-states use criminal proxies; criminals buy nation-state tools).
- **Insider blind spots** — internal actors are the hardest to detect.

---

## 11. Real-World Examples

- **Nation-state:** APT groups stealing defense/research IP (espionage), or attacking power grids (war/service disruption).
- **Organized crime:** ransomware-as-a-service crews extorting companies (financial gain + blackmail).
- **Hacktivist:** groups defacing or DDoSing organizations they oppose (political beliefs).
- **Insider:** a departing employee copying the customer database (revenge / financial gain).
- **Unskilled attacker:** someone running a downloaded DDoS tool against a game server (chaos).
- **Shadow IT:** a marketing team spinning up an unapproved SaaS app holding customer data (uncontrolled risk).

---

## 12. Enterprise Usage (SOC relevance)

- **Triage context:** identifying the likely actor shapes how you investigate and escalate (a suspected nation-state APT is a far bigger deal than an unskilled scanner).
- **Insider threat** is a core SOC focus — UEBA, DLP, and access reviews target it.
- **Shadow IT** appears as unknown assets/traffic in your monitoring — discovering and reporting it is part of the job.
- **Threat intel feeds** map indicators to actors so you know who you're dealing with.

---

## 13. Security Implications

- **Nation-state/APT:** long dwell time, stealth, advanced techniques — assume persistence.
- **Organized crime:** ransomware can halt the business; data theft has financial/legal fallout.
- **Insider:** bypasses perimeter controls; needs internal monitoring and least privilege.
- **Shadow IT:** unmanaged, unpatched, unmonitored systems = silent attack surface.
- **Unskilled attacker:** high volume, low skill — basic hygiene blocks most of it.

---

## 14. Attacker / Adversary Perspective

- Actors choose targets by **payoff vs. effort**: criminals chase money, nation-states chase secrets/strategic advantage.
- **Resources determine reach:** a nation-state can burn a zero-day; an unskilled attacker reuses public exploits.
- Sophisticated actors **blend in** (living-off-the-land, valid accounts) to avoid the very indicators you watch for (ties to 2.4).
- Insiders exploit **trust** — they already have access, so they skip the break-in.

---

## 15. Defender Perspective

- **Threat-model by actor:** who realistically targets your industry and assets?
- **Layer defenses** to raise cost above the actor's willingness/ability to pay.
- **Insider controls:** least privilege, separation of duties, monitoring, offboarding.
- **Shadow IT controls:** discovery (CASB, scans), policy, sanctioned alternatives.
- **Threat intel:** track the actors and TTPs relevant to you.

---

## 16. Detection Techniques

- **Threat intelligence + IOCs** mapped to known actor groups.
- **UEBA** for insider/anomalous behavior.
- **Network discovery / CASB** for shadow IT.
- **Honeypots/deception** to surface and characterize attackers.
- **Long-dwell hunting** for APT-style stealth (nation-states).

---

## 17. Mitigation Techniques

- **Least privilege + separation of duties** (insiders).
- **Defense in depth** to raise attacker cost (all actors).
- **Patching + hardening** to stop unskilled attackers and many criminals (2.5).
- **CASB / sanctioned tools** for shadow IT.
- **Strong access control + MFA** to blunt credential-based attacks.

---

## 18. Best Practices

1. **Know your likely adversaries** (industry-specific threat modeling).
2. **Run an insider-threat program** (monitoring + least privilege + offboarding).
3. **Discover and govern shadow IT** rather than ignoring it.
4. **Consume threat intelligence** relevant to your sector.
5. **Layer controls** so low-skill actors are stopped cheaply and high-skill actors are slowed.
6. **Map motivations to assets** — protect what your likely actors want most.

## 21. Exam Tips

- **"Financially motivated + professional/organized"** → Organized crime.
- **"Promoting a political/social cause"** → Hacktivist.

- **"Unsanctioned/unapproved IT used by employees"** → Shadow IT.

- **Shadow IT is internal and usually not malicious** — the risk is lack of control, not intent.

## 22. Common CompTIA Question Styles

1. *"A well-funded group backed by a government conducts a stealthy, long-term campaign to steal research."* → Nation-state (motivation: espionage).
2. *"A criminal group deploys ransomware to extort companies for profit."* → Organized crime (financial gain).
3. *"An activist group defaces a company's site to protest its policies."* → Hacktivist (political beliefs).
4. *"A departing employee downloads confidential files out of spite."* → Insider threat (revenge).
5. *"Someone with little technical skill launches an attack using a tool they downloaded."* → Unskilled attacker.
6. *"Employees set up an unapproved cloud service to share files."* → Shadow IT.
7. *"Which attribute best distinguishes a nation-state from an unskilled attacker?"* → Resources/funding and sophistication.

**Why distractors fail:** they swap actors with overlapping motives. Anchor on the *distinguishing* attribute (funding/skill/internal-external) and the *primary* motive named in the scenario.

---

## 23. Interview Notes

- *"What's an APT?"* — An advanced persistent threat: a sophisticated, well-resourced actor (often nation-state) maintaining long-term stealthy access.
- *"Why are insider threats so dangerous?"* — They have legitimate access, bypass the perimeter, and know where the valuable data is.
- *"What is shadow IT and why is it a risk?"* — Unsanctioned IT used without approval; it's unmanaged, unpatched, and unmonitored, expanding the attack surface.
- *"How do motivations help defenders?"* — They predict targets: espionage actors go for IP, financial actors for payment data.

---

## 24. Common Beginner Mistakes

1. **Confusing hacktivist (cause) with organized crime (money).**
2. **Calling shadow IT an external hacker** — it's internal, usually non-malicious.
3. **Assuming all insiders are malicious** — many incidents are negligent/unintentional.
4. **Treating "unskilled attacker" as harmless** — they're high-volume and can still cause damage with others' tools.
5. **Mixing up the three attributes** (internal/external vs. resources vs. sophistication).
6. **Forgetting "ethical" is a non-malicious motivation.**

## 29. Practice Questions

**Q1.** A highly sophisticated, government-funded group conducts a multi-year stealth campaign to steal a defense contractor's research. Which threat actor is this?
A. Hacktivist  B. Unskilled attacker  C. Nation-state  D. Shadow IT

**Q2.** A professional criminal organization deploys ransomware purely to make money. What is their primary motivation?
A. Philosophical beliefs  B. Financial gain  C. Revenge  D. Ethical

**Q3.** Employees begin using an unapproved cloud file-sharing service to collaborate, bypassing IT. This best describes:
A. Insider threat  B. Organized crime  C. Shadow IT  D. Hacktivist

**Q4.** Which two threat actors are INTERNAL?
A. Nation-state and hacktivist
B. Insider threat and shadow IT
C. Organized crime and insider threat
D. Unskilled attacker and shadow IT

**Q5.** An attacker with little technical ability launches a DDoS using a tool they downloaded online. This actor is a(n):
A. Nation-state  B. Unskilled attacker  C. Organized crime  D. Insider threat

**Q6.** A group defaces government websites to protest a new law. Their motivation is best described as:
A. Financial gain  B. Espionage  C. Philosophical/political beliefs  D. War

**Q7.** Which attribute most clearly separates a nation-state actor from an unskilled attacker?
A. Internal vs. external  B. Resources/funding and sophistication  C. Geographic location  D. Choice of programming language

**Q8.** A disgruntled employee about to be fired copies sensitive customer data to sell. This is an insider threat motivated primarily by:
A. Ethical reasons  B. Revenge and/or financial gain  C. War  D. Service disruption

**Q9.** Which motivation is associated with authorized, non-malicious activity?
A. Blackmail  B. Espionage  C. Ethical  D. Disruption/chaos

**Q10.** An "advanced persistent threat" (APT) is most associated with which actor?
A. Unskilled attacker  B. Shadow IT  C. Nation-state  D. Hacktivist

### Answer Key & Explanations

- **Q1 — C (Nation-state).** Sophisticated + government-funded + long-term stealth = nation-state/APT (espionage).
- **Q2 — B (Financial gain).** Organized crime's defining motive.
- **Q3 — C (Shadow IT).** Unsanctioned IT used by employees; internal, risk-creating.
- **Q4 — B.** Insider threat and shadow IT are the two internal actors.
- **Q5 — B (Unskilled attacker).** Low skill, uses others' tools.
- **Q6 — C (Political beliefs).** Hacktivist motivation.
- **Q7 — B.** Resources/funding and sophistication separate them most clearly.
- **Q8 — B (Revenge and/or financial gain).** Classic malicious-insider motives.
- **Q9 — C (Ethical).** White-hat/authorized motivation.
- **Q10 — C (Nation-state).** APTs are typically nation-state actors.
