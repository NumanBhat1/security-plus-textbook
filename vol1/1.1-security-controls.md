## 4. Concept Overview

A **security control** is any safeguard or countermeasure put in place to reduce risk to the confidentiality, integrity, or availability (CIA) of an asset. Controls are the *mechanisms* through which security policy becomes reality. Without controls, a security policy is just a document; controls are how the organization actually does something about a threat.

CompTIA wants you to classify controls along **two independent axes**:

- **Category** answers *"What kind of thing is this control?"* — is it technology, a management decision, a human process, or a physical barrier?
- **Type (function)** answers *"What does this control DO relative to an attack?"* — does it stop an attack, scare off an attacker, notice an attack, fix the damage, substitute for a missing control, or direct behavior?

These two axes are **orthogonal** — every real control has *both* a category and a type. A door lock is **Physical** (category) and **Preventive** (type). A SIEM alert is **Technical** (category) and **Detective** (type). Mastering this objective means being able to place any control on both axes at once.

```
                 CONTROL TYPE (what it does)
                 Preventive Deterrent Detective Corrective Compensating Directive

   T                                                              transfer ] mandate ]
```

## 5. Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Security control** | A safeguard or countermeasure that reduces risk to an asset's confidentiality, integrity, or availability. |
| **Control category** | The *nature* of a control — Technical, Managerial, Operational, or Physical. |
| **Control type (functional type)** | The *function* a control performs against a threat — Preventive, Deterrent, Detective, Corrective, Compensating, or Directive. |
| **Technical control** | Also called a *logical* control; implemented through technology/hardware/software (firewalls, encryption, MFA). |
| **Managerial control** | Also called an *administrative* control; implemented through management decisions, policies, and oversight (risk assessments, security policies). |
| **Operational control** | A control executed primarily by **people** carrying out day-to-day processes (security guards, awareness training, log review). |
| **Physical control** | A control that protects the physical environment and tangible assets (locks, fences, badges, cameras). |
| **Preventive control** | Stops an incident *before* it happens. |
| **Deterrent control** | Discourages an attacker from attempting an attack (psychological). |
| **Detective control** | Identifies and records that an incident is occurring or has occurred. |
| **Corrective control** | Restores systems and limits damage *after* an incident. |
| **Compensating control** | An alternative control used when the primary/ideal control is not feasible. |
| **Directive control** | Directs, guides, or mandates behavior toward compliance (policies, signage, procedures). |
| **Defense in depth** | Layering multiple controls so that the failure of one does not result in compromise. |

## 6. Deep Technical Explanation

The reason CompTIA splits controls into **category** and **type** is that they answer fundamentally different questions, and real security programs are designed using *both*.

**Category** is about *implementation domain*. When an auditor or architect builds a control framework, they need to assign ownership: a **Technical** control is owned by engineering/IT, a **Managerial** control is owned by governance/leadership, an **Operational** control is owned by the people running daily operations, and a **Physical** control is owned by facilities/physical security. Categorization drives *who is responsible* and *how the control is maintained*.

**Type** is about *position in the attack timeline*. Think of an incident as a sequence: before → during → after. Controls are designed to act at different points:

```
   TIMELINE OF AN INCIDENT
   ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────►
   BEFORE the attack        DURING the attack        AFTER the attack
   ┌──────────────┐         ┌──────────────┐         ┌──────────────┐
   │ Preventive   │         │ Detective    │         │ Corrective   │
   │ Deterrent    │         │              │         │              │
   │ Directive    │         │              │         │              │
   └──────────────┘         └──────────────┘         └──────────────┘
   (Compensating sits across the whole timeline — it substitutes for
    whatever control is missing, so it can be preventive-compensating,
    detective-compensating, etc.)
```

- **Preventive** and **Deterrent** and **Directive** mostly act *before*.
- **Detective** acts *during/at the moment of* the event (it sees it happen).
- **Corrective** acts *after* (it cleans up).
- **Compensating** is special: it is defined by *circumstance*, not timeline. It is whatever you put in place because the control you *wanted* isn't available. A compensating control can itself be preventive, detective, etc. — its defining quality is "substitute for the ideal control."

A subtle but exam-critical point: **Deterrent vs. Preventive**. A deterrent works on the *attacker's mind* ("a guard dog sign makes me not try"), whereas a preventive control works on the *attack mechanism* ("the locked door physically stops me even if I try"). A warning banner is deterrent; the access control that actually blocks the login is preventive. The same physical object can even do both — a tall fence *deters* (looks hard to climb) and *prevents* (is hard to climb).

Another exam-critical pair: **Detective vs. Corrective**. Detective *notices* (CCTV records the break-in, IDS logs the intrusion). Corrective *fixes* (restoring from backup, applying a patch, quarantining malware). A camera that only records is detective; a system that automatically isolates an infected host is corrective (it took action to limit damage).

## 7. Why It Exists

Security controls exist because **risk cannot be eliminated, only managed**. Every asset faces threats; controls are the levers an organization pulls to bring residual risk down to an acceptable level (its *risk tolerance* — see Objective 5.2).

The category/type taxonomy exists because:

- **Frameworks and auditors need a common language.** NIST 800-53, ISO 27001, and CIS Controls all classify safeguards so organizations can prove coverage. If you can't categorize a control, you can't demonstrate to a regulator that you've addressed a requirement.
- **Gap analysis depends on it** (see 1.2). To find what's missing, you map existing controls against required controls — which requires a classification scheme.
- **Defense in depth requires variety.** If every control you own is the same type (all preventive, say), then once prevention fails you have nothing. A mature program intentionally spreads controls across *all* types and *all* categories so a single failure isn't catastrophic.

---

## 8. How It Works

In practice, an organization builds a **control set** through this loop:

```
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ 1. Identify assets and the risks to them                 │
   │ 2. Select controls (category + type) to address each risk│
   │ 3. Implement controls (owned per category)               │
   │ 4. Monitor effectiveness (detective controls help here)  │
   │ 5. Reassess — gap analysis finds missing coverage        │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                         ▲                       │
                         └───────── feedback ────┘
```

For any single risk, a well-designed program layers types:

> **Risk: An attacker steals an employee laptop.**
> - **Directive:** Policy requiring full-disk encryption on all laptops.
> - **Preventive:** Full-disk encryption (technical) + cable lock (physical).
> - **Deterrent:** Asset tags + "monitored" stickers.
> - **Detective:** MDM reports the device offline / geolocation.
> - **Corrective:** Remote wipe; reissue device from backup image.
> - **Compensating:** If a legacy laptop can't run FDE, require it never leave the building (an alternative control).

---

## 9. Advantages

- **Comprehensive coverage:** Classifying by category + type ensures no blind spots (you can see if you have only preventive controls and no detective ones).
- **Clear ownership:** Category assigns responsibility (IT vs. management vs. ops vs. facilities).
- **Auditability:** Maps cleanly to compliance frameworks.
- **Resilience:** Encourages defense in depth — layered, varied controls.
- **Cost optimization:** Compensating controls let you stay secure when the ideal control is unaffordable or infeasible.

---

## 10. Disadvantages

- **Classification ambiguity:** Many controls fit multiple types (a fence is deterrent *and* preventive), which confuses learners and can spark audit debate.
- **Overlap and redundancy cost money:** Layering everything is expensive; organizations must balance coverage against budget.
- **False sense of security:** Having a control checkbox "ticked" doesn't mean it's *effective* — a poorly configured firewall is still a "technical preventive control" on paper.
- **Compensating controls can become permanent crutches:** Teams sometimes accept a weaker compensating control indefinitely instead of fixing the root issue (technical debt — see 4.7).

| Control | Category | Type | Why |
|---------|----------|------|-----|
| Firewall | Technical | Preventive | Technology that blocks unauthorized traffic before it lands. |

| IDS (alert only) | Technical | Detective | Notices and logs malicious traffic; doesn't block it. |
| IPS (blocks) | Technical | Preventive | Notices *and stops* the traffic. |
| Security policy document | Managerial | Directive | Management mandate guiding behavior. |
| Risk assessment | Managerial | Detective (of risk) | Identifies where risk exists. |
| Cyber-insurance | Managerial | Compensating | Transfers/offsets residual risk when you can't fully prevent it. |

| Security awareness training | Operational | Directive / Preventive | People-process that guides and reduces user error. |

| "Beware of Dog" / CCTV-in-use sign | Physical | Deterrent | Discourages attempt psychologically. |

## 12. Enterprise Usage

In a real enterprise, controls are documented in a **control matrix** mapped to a framework (NIST CSF, ISO 27001 Annex A, or CIS Controls). Each control entry typically records: the control ID, description, **category**, **type**, owner, implementation status, and the risk(s) it addresses. A SOC analyst encounters this when:

- **Responding to audits:** "Show me your detective controls for unauthorized access." → You point to SIEM, IDS, log review.
- **Designing detections:** Detection engineering (Volume 7) is essentially building *detective technical controls*.
- **Incident response:** Containment and eradication steps are *corrective controls* in action.
- **Recommending fixes post-incident:** A good analyst recommends controls across multiple types (not just "patch it" — also "add monitoring" and "update the policy").

---

## 13. Security Implications

- An organization heavy on **preventive** controls but light on **detective** ones will *be breached and not know it* — the worst position, because dwell time (how long an attacker stays undetected) is the single biggest driver of breach cost.
- Over-reliance on **deterrent** controls (which only affect attacker psychology) gives no real protection against a determined or automated adversary — bots don't read warning banners.
- **Compensating** controls left in place too long become *systemic weaknesses* attackers map and target.
- The right posture is **balanced and layered**: deter where you can, prevent what you can, detect everything, and be ready to correct.

---

## 14. Attacker Perspective

Attackers actively reason about your controls:

- They **probe for preventive gaps** (open ports, missing patches, weak auth) because prevention is the first wall.
- They **assume deterrents are bluffs** — a sophisticated actor isn't scared off by a sign; deterrents mostly stop opportunistic, low-skill attackers.
- They **try to evade or disable detective controls** — clearing logs (Windows Event ID 1102), disabling EDR, using "living off the land" techniques to blend in. (This is why *missing logs* is itself an indicator of compromise — see 2.4.)
- They **race the corrective controls** — encrypt data faster than you can restore from backup (ransomware), or exfiltrate before you can isolate.
- They look for **compensating controls that are weaker than the real thing** and target those as the soft underbelly.

The attacker's goal is to operate inside the *gaps between* your controls. The defender's goal is to leave no such gap.

---

## 15. Defender Perspective

A defender (you, the SOC analyst) uses the taxonomy as a **mental checklist**:

- *"Do I have prevention here?"* (firewall, MFA, patching)
- *"If prevention fails, will I detect it?"* (logging, SIEM, IDS/EDR)
- *"If detected, can I correct it fast?"* (IR playbooks, backups, isolation)
- *"Is anything held together by a compensating control I should upgrade?"*
- *"Does policy (directive) actually back up the technical controls?"*

Defenders **layer types deliberately** so a single failure isn't fatal — this is *defense in depth*. They also recognize that detective controls are their early-warning system; investing in detection is what shrinks attacker dwell time.

---

## 16. Detection Techniques

This objective *is partly about* detection (the Detective control type). For the broader "how do I detect things" skill set:

- **Detective technical controls:** SIEM correlation, IDS/IPS in detect mode, EDR/XDR telemetry, file integrity monitoring (FIM), NetFlow analysis, antivirus alerts. (Tools detailed in 4.4.)
- **Detective operational controls:** Analyst log review, threat hunting, periodic access reviews.
- **Detective physical controls:** CCTV, motion/IR/pressure sensors, door-open logs from badge systems.
- **Key metric:** detective controls are measured by *coverage* (do we collect the right logs?) and *time-to-detect*.

A practical link to your own work: the brute-force log parser and phishing analyzer in your `python-security-tools` repo are exactly *detective technical controls*.

---

## 17. Mitigation Techniques

Mitigation maps to the **Preventive** and **Corrective** types:

- **Preventive mitigation:** hardening (4.1), patching (2.5), least privilege (4.6), segmentation (2.5), encryption (1.4), MFA (4.6).
- **Corrective mitigation:** patch deployment after a vuln is found, malware quarantine/removal, restoring from backup, rebuilding compromised hosts, password resets after credential theft.
- **Compensating mitigation:** when you can't apply the ideal fix (e.g., a legacy ICS system that can't be patched — see 3.1), you wrap it in segmentation + monitoring as an alternative.

---

## 18. Best Practices

1. **Classify every control on both axes** (category *and* type) in your control matrix.
2. **Aim for balance across all six types** — never all-preventive.
3. **Prioritize detection** — you cannot respond to what you cannot see.
4. **Treat compensating controls as temporary** with a documented plan to reach the ideal control.
5. **Map controls to a recognized framework** (NIST/ISO/CIS) for auditability.
6. **Assign an owner per control** based on category.
7. **Test controls regularly** — a control that isn't verified may have silently failed.
8. **Layer (defense in depth)** so one control's failure is caught by another.

## 21. Exam Tips

- **Read for "what does it DO" to find the type, and "what KIND of thing is it" to find the category.** CompTIA questions usually ask for one axis at a time.
- A **camera that only records** = **Detective**, not preventive. A camera *plus a visible sign* adds **Deterrent**.
- **IDS = Detective; IPS = Preventive.** (IDS detects; IPS prevents.) This single distinction shows up constantly.
- **Backups/restore = Corrective.** Encryption = Preventive. Don't confuse them.
- **Policy/AUP** is usually **Managerial + Directive**.
- **Guard/training delivery = Operational** (people). The *policy requiring* training is **Managerial**.
- **Compensating** is the answer when the question says *"the ideal control isn't possible, so they did X instead."*
- **"Discourage" / "dissuade" / "make them think twice"** → **Deterrent**.
- **"Restore," "recover," "fix," "limit damage after"** → **Corrective**.

## 22. Common CompTIA Question Styles

1. **Single-axis classification:** *"A company installs bollards in front of its building. What TYPE of control is this?"* → Preventive (and could be deterrent; pick the best per wording — bollards physically stop, so Preventive).
2. **Both-axis classification:** *"Which BEST describes a host-based firewall?"* → Technical, Preventive.
3. **Scenario substitution (compensating):** *"A legacy server cannot be patched. The team isolates it on a separate VLAN and monitors it. What control type is the VLAN isolation acting as?"* → Compensating.
4. **Distractor traps:** Offering "Detective" when the control clearly *acts* (that's Corrective), or "Preventive" when it only *discourages* (that's Deterrent).
5. **Category confusion:** Offering "Operational" vs. "Managerial" for a policy — the *written policy* is Managerial/Directive; *people carrying out a daily process* is Operational.

**Why wrong answers are wrong:** CompTIA distractors usually swap an adjacent concept — Detective for Corrective, Deterrent for Preventive, Managerial for Operational. Identify the *defining verb* in the scenario ("records" vs. "fixes" vs. "discourages" vs. "blocks") to eliminate them.

---

## 23. Interview Notes

Interviewers may ask:

- *"What's the difference between a detective and a preventive control?"* — Show you know prevention stops the event, detection notices it; mention that detection shrinks dwell time.
- *"Give an example of a compensating control you'd recommend for an unpatchable system."* — Strong answer: network segmentation + enhanced monitoring + strict ACLs, with a documented plan to remediate.
- *"After an incident, what controls would you recommend?"* — A senior-sounding answer spans multiple types: preventive (patch/harden), detective (add logging/alerting), and directive (update the policy). Recommending *only* "patch it" signals junior thinking.
- They want to hear **layering / defense in depth** as your instinct.

---

## 24. Common Beginner Mistakes

1. Thinking a control has *only one* type — many have two (a fence deters *and* prevents).
2. Calling a **CCTV camera preventive** — recording alone is **detective**.
3. Confusing **Managerial** (the policy) with **Operational** (people executing daily tasks).
4. Treating **Deterrent** as real protection — it only affects attacker psychology.
5. Mixing **Detective** and **Corrective** — "notices" vs. "fixes."
6. Forgetting **Compensating** is defined by *circumstance* (substitute), not by timeline.
7. Believing a checked-box control is automatically *effective* — controls must be tested.

## 27. Key Facts

- A security control reduces risk to **CIA**.
- **Two axes:** Category (what kind) and Type (what it does). Every control has both.
- **4 categories:** Technical (logical), Managerial (administrative), Operational (people), Physical.
- **6 types:** Preventive, Deterrent, Detective, Corrective, Compensating, Directive.
- **IDS = detective, IPS = preventive.**

- **Compensating = substitute for an unavailable ideal control.**
- **Directive = mandates/guides behavior (policies, AUP, signage that instructs).**
- **Defense in depth = layer multiple types so one failure isn't fatal.**

## 29. Practice Questions

**Q1.** A data center installs a sign reading "24/7 Video Surveillance." The sign by itself is which control type?
A. Preventive  B. Detective  C. Deterrent  D. Corrective

**Q2.** Which pairing is correct?
A. Encryption — Corrective
B. Backup restoration — Preventive
C. IDS — Detective
D. Warning banner — Preventive

**Q3.** A legacy industrial controller cannot be patched. Security places it on an isolated VLAN with strict ACLs and continuous monitoring. The VLAN isolation is best described as a ______ control.
A. Directive  B. Compensating  C. Deterrent  D. Detective

**Q4.** An organization's Acceptable Use Policy is which *category* and *type*?
A. Technical / Preventive
B. Managerial / Directive
C. Operational / Detective
D. Physical / Deterrent

**Q5.** Which control TYPE shrinks attacker dwell time the most by surfacing activity that already bypassed prevention?
A. Preventive  B. Deterrent  C. Detective  D. Directive

**Q6.** A security guard who is clearly visible, watches monitors, and can physically stop intruders performs which control types? (Choose the best single answer for "watches monitors.")
A. Preventive  B. Detective  C. Corrective  D. Compensating

**Q7.** Which is a *Technical, Preventive* control?
A. CCTV camera  B. Multifactor authentication  C. Security awareness training  D. Cyber-insurance

**Q8.** Match the verb to the type: "The system automatically isolates the infected host and restores the clean image." This is primarily ______.
A. Detective  B. Deterrent  C. Corrective  D. Directive

### Answer Key & Explanations

- **Q1 — C (Deterrent).** A sign affects the attacker's *decision*; it doesn't record (detective) or block (preventive). The *camera* would be detective, but the *sign alone* is deterrent.
- **Q2 — C (IDS — Detective).** IDS detects/alerts; it doesn't block (that's IPS). A: encryption is preventive. B: restoration is corrective. D: a warning banner is deterrent.
- **Q3 — B (Compensating).** The ideal control (patching) is impossible, so an alternative (isolation+monitoring) substitutes — the definition of compensating.
- **Q4 — B (Managerial / Directive).** A policy is a management mandate (managerial) that *directs* behavior (directive).
- **Q5 — C (Detective).** Detection surfaces activity that got past prevention, reducing dwell time.
- **Q6 — B (Detective).** "Watches monitors" = noticing/observing. (The guard *also* deters and can prevent, but the specific act described is detective.)
- **Q7 — B (MFA).** Technology (technical) that blocks unauthorized access before it happens (preventive). A is physical/detective; C is operational; D is managerial/compensating.
- **Q8 — C (Corrective).** "Isolates" and "restores" are actions that fix/limit damage after the event.

## 30. PBQ-style Scenario

> **Scenario:** You are a SOC analyst. Leadership asks you to classify the following controls into a matrix (Category + Type) for an upcoming ISO 27001 audit. Fill in each.

| # | Control | Category | Type |
|---|---------|----------|------|
| 1 | Next-generation firewall blocking malicious IPs | ? | ? |
| 2 | Quarterly security awareness training delivered to staff | ? | ? |
| 3 | "Authorized Personnel Only" sign on a server room door | ? | ? |
| 4 | Badge reader that logs every door entry | ? | ? |
| 5 | Nightly encrypted backups used to restore after ransomware | ? | ? |
| 6 | Cyber-insurance policy covering breach costs | ? | ? |
| 7 | Company Acceptable Use Policy | ? | ? |
| 8 | Legacy app can't use MFA, so access is restricted to one hardened jump server | ? | ? |

**Answer key:**

1. **Technical / Preventive** — technology that blocks before the event.
2. **Operational / Directive (or Preventive)** — people-process that guides behavior and reduces user error.
3. **Physical / Deterrent** — a physical sign that discourages.
4. **Physical / Detective** — physical device that records/notices entries.
5. **Technical / Corrective** — technology used to restore/limit damage after the incident.
6. **Managerial / Compensating** — a management mechanism offsetting residual risk you can't fully prevent (also described as risk *transfer*).
7. **Managerial / Directive** — a management mandate directing behavior.
8. **Technical / Compensating** — an alternative to the ideal control (MFA) that isn't feasible.
